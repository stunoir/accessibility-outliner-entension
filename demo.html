The quiet revolution in how we read on screens

By Polly Tompkins · 22 May 2026

For decades the page was a fixed thing. Ink met paper and stayed there. The screen changed that contract quietly, and we are only now reckoning with what it means to read a sentence that can reflow, resize, and rearrange itself beneath our gaze.

How spacing shapes attention

Typographers have always known that the space around words matters as much as the words themselves. On screens, that space is negotiable - and the people who set it are not always the people who read it. Recent studies suggest the gap between comfortable and exhausting can be a matter of millimetres.

When a heading is left blank, assistive technology announces a heading with no name, which is disorienting for anyone navigating by structure. The paragraph that follows looks fine, but the signpost above it points nowhere.

Good reading design is mostly invisible. You only notice it when it fails.

The trouble with icons

Publishers love a compact toolbar. But a row of unlabelled icons is a row of locked doors for a screen reader user. Below, the share controls have no accessible names at all - and they are far too small to tap reliably.
